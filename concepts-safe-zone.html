Safe Zone — UX Concept Exploration

8 concept directions, each with 3 variations — so you can see the trade-offs and understand why one approach wins per category.

Canvas Boundary Safe Zone 8 Concepts · 3 Variants Each Print Production

The Problem

Users don't instinctively understand the difference between the digital canvas and the physical sticker sheet — leading to content that gets cut off in production.

How to Read This

Each concept card shows 3 ways to execute the same underlying idea. The ★ Recommended column is the strongest execution of that concept, with rationale.

Constraints

Mobile canvas is small. Guides must not clutter the working area, serve both first-time and power users, and map to real print outcomes.

PA 1 Foundational — defines how users read and trust the physical canvas

Physical Canvas Mental Model

Canvas Boundaries & Safe Zones

Reason

Users don't know what will physically print vs. what gets cut off. This system is the invisible foundation every other canvas interaction depends on.

Possible Solutions

Persistent frame · Dimmed overflow · Color-coded zones · Smart warnings · Selection guides · Onboarding · Artboard/pasteboard · Dual guide toggle

Concept 01
Elevated Artboard · Spatial Canvas Boundary
The sticker sheet floats above the workspace surface through a material elevation shadow. No labels or overlaid borders — the artboard's default white reflects the physical sheet color, and the gray workspace surround provides natural contrast. The boundary is spatial, not graphical.
4 × 7 in
★ Recommended
Slightly Elevated Artboard
No visual noise — the artboard stays completely clean for artwork; the shadow is subconscious, not a design element
Sheet color reflects physical material — white for standard paper, automatically adapts to other sheet types (kraft, clear, holographic)
Requires a visible gray workspace surround at all times — if the artboard is zoomed to fill the screen, the elevation cue disappears
Shadow alone carries no semantic label — first-time users may not connect "lifted card" to "print boundary" without onboarding
Conflicts with PA2-C04 (Pinch-focus zoom): when the artboard is zoomed to fill the screen, the gray workspace surrounding it disappears — and with it, the only boundary signal. Focus mode needs a fallback indicator (e.g. a faint edge highlight) to replace the elevation cue.

Stronger white-to-gray contrast makes the artboard boundary legible even at a quick glance — especially useful at smaller zoom levels
Dark workspace recedes visually, keeping user focus entirely on the artboard and the work on it
Darker workspace may feel heavy or industrial for a consumer-facing product — less friendly than a neutral mid-gray
High contrast creates a "spotlight" feeling that may be fatiguing during long editing sessions
Conflicts with PA1-C06 (Spotlight Onboarding): both concepts use a dark surround and white-highlighted artboard. If both are in the product, the persistent dark workspace and the onboarding spotlight look identical — users cannot tell if they're in an "intro mode" or normal editing state.

Elevation + Sheet Material Tint
Artboard color = physical sheet — users designing on kraft see a kraft-tinted canvas, immediately understanding what their sticker will look like on the actual material
Eliminates a common print surprise: "my colors look different on the actual sticker" — the canvas is a live material preview
Requires sheet material selection to be a first-time setup step — if users skip it, the artboard defaults to white regardless of their actual sheet order
Very dark sheet materials (black, navy) would render sticker artwork nearly invisible on the canvas — needs an alternate preview mode

Concept 02
Dimmed Overflow Region
The workspace surrounding the artboard is a neutral gray — the white artboard and gray surround define the boundary through tonal contrast alone, without any overlay label or frame. This is already the behavior in the current Cricut Design Space. Variants explore contrast level and overflow treatment.
★ Recommended
Light Gray Workspace
Already implemented — zero engineering change; users familiar with the existing app already understand this model
Non-intrusive: the artboard is fully clean for artwork, the boundary is felt as a surface, not read as a label
Very light gray may be nearly invisible on bright displays or in high-ambient-light environments
New users who've never used a design tool may treat the gray workspace as usable canvas — no clear "this doesn't print" signal
Conflicts with PA3-C01 (Fully Constrained Canvas): if elements can never leave the artboard, the gray workspace always stays empty — it looks like unused dead space and users may attempt to drag elements into it, not understanding it's off-limits.
Darker Neutral Workspace
High contrast — artboard reads as clearly white even on bright displays; boundary unmissable at a quick glance
Gray is dark enough that no user would mistake it for an editable canvas area
Darker surround feels heavier; may read as "disabled" or "locked" to some users
Strong contrast may be fatiguing during longer editing sessions
Conflicts with C01-B (Elevation + Darkened Workspace): both use a dark gray surround on a white artboard. If these concepts converge, the team needs a single decision — elevation shadow or tonal contrast — or the combined output risks looking redundant.

Workspace with Off-Canvas Ghost
Workspace is not "dead space" — it actively shows elements that have drifted off the artboard, making them easy to recover
Ghost treatment (faded silhouette) communicates "here but not printing" without alarming the user
Requires runtime compositing: the app must render a ghosted version of each off-canvas element in the workspace layer
Users may see the ghost and think the element printed fine — unclear whether it's a warning or a feature

PA 2 Fundamental interaction pattern used throughout the product

Focus Mode Interaction

How users enter, navigate, and exit focused editing states on canvas elements

Reason

Every element interaction — moving, resizing, editing color, editing text — depends on how users enter and exit focus mode. Getting this wrong creates confusion product-wide.

Possible Solutions

Double-tap enter/exit · Single-select + Edit button · Breadcrumb navigation · Contextual zoom/focus states

Deliverables

Focus mode interaction model · Gesture standards · Interaction flow diagrams · Conflict matrix with other features

PA 3 Directly impacts every creation flow

Canvas vs. Artboard Paradigm

Decide whether users can place content outside the physical canvas for staging or organization

UX Need

Users naturally want to stage, compare, and park elements. The workspace model determines if that's possible — and shapes how every drag, drop, and placement interaction feels.

Possible Solutions

Fully constrained canvas · Infinite staging area · Temporary "parking lot" region · Hidden asset tray

Deliverables

Recommendation on workspace model · Object behavior specifications · Edge-case handling for off-canvas elements
